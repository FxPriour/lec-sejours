State of the tournament — Friday 27 June

Theme: what we know & what we don't · Grammar: present perfect (confirmed) vs. modals/conditional (undecided)
🎲 Quick start
Vote with your feet — TRUE / FALSE: read a few statements from the table ("Brazil have qualified."). Pupils move, then say if it is certain or still not sure.

Reading — A tournament still being decided

At the time of writing, on the evening of Friday 27 June, the group stage was nearly over — but not quite. Many teams have already qualified for the Round of 32. Brazil, France, Argentina, Germany, the Netherlands and the host nations Mexico, the USA and Canada are all through.

But some places have not been decided yet. A few groups still had matches to play, and the eight "best third-placed teams" were not all known. Scotland, for example, might still go through — or they could be knocked out. It all depended on the last few matches.
Written on 27 June 2026 — some results were still unknown.
  • ★ Name two teams that have already qualified.
  • ★★ Why does the writer say "but not quite"?
  • ★★★ Find two ways the text shows that something is not certain.
Answers
1) Any two of: Brazil, France, Argentina, Germany, Netherlands, Mexico, USA, Canada. 2) The group stage was "nearly" over — a few matches remained. 3) e.g. "might still go through", "could be knocked out", "had not been decided yet", "depended on the last few matches".

Grammar focus — certain vs. not certain

Two columns, two grammars
Certain (it happened)Not certain (it may happen)
"Brazil have qualified." (present perfect)"Scotland might qualify." (modal)
"France won their group." (past simple)"They could still go through." / "It depends on tonight's match."
★★ Certain or not? — sort & say
Your teacher reads a statement about a team. Decide: certain or not certain? Then say it again with the right grammar.
StatementCertain ✅ / Not sure ❓Say it correctly
★ "Brazil have qualified." (sure) ★★★ "If Scotland win tonight, they will go through." (condition)
Setup
Read a mix of confirmed facts and open ones. Push ★★★ pupils towards first conditional ("If… they will…").
