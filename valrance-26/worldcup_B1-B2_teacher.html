Reading — The biggest World Cup ever

The 2026 World Cup has rewritten the tournament's scale. For the first time it is co-hosted by three nations — the United States, Mexico and Canada — and for the first time it features 48 teams rather than 32, producing a record 104 matches across 16 host cities. The expansion also introduced a brand-new knockout round, the Round of 32, which never existed before: teams no longer move straight from the group stage into the Round of 16.

The geographical spread is unprecedented. Matches stretch from Vancouver on the Pacific coast to Miami on the Atlantic, and south to Mexico City, more than 2,000 kilometres away. For some fixtures, that means long flights, multiple time zones, and very different climates — a logistical challenge that previous, single-country tournaments never faced.
Adapted from FIFA & BBC Sport, June 2026
  • ★ Name two "firsts" mentioned in the text.
  • ★★ What is the Round of 32, and why is it new?
  • ★★★ The writer calls the geography "a logistical challenge". Using the text, explain two concrete problems this could cause a team.
Answers
1) Any two: first three-nation co-host; first 48-team edition; first Round of 32. 2) An extra knockout round inserted between the group stage and the Round of 16, created because 32 teams (not 16) now advance. 3) e.g. long-haul travel/fatigue; crossing time zones disrupting rest; adapting to very different climates (Vancouver vs. Mexico City heat/altitude).

Day 2 — Knockout drama

Theme: the psychology of knockout football · Grammar: degrees of prediction & hedging
🎲 Move & speak — 2 games to start
1 · Would You Rather — debate version — Provocative pairs ("Would you rather your team won ugly or lost beautifully?"). Pupils choose a side and build a 30-second case.
Argument + rebuttal practice.
2 · Four Corners — calibrated certainty — Corners: Likely / Possible / Unlikely / No idea. Announce a fixture; pupils place themselves and phrase a prediction whose language matches the corner (may well / is likely to / I doubt…).
This IS the hedging objective, in motion.

Reading — The cruelty of penalties

There is no crueller way to lose a football match than a penalty shoot-out. After 120 minutes — 90 plus extra time — two exhausted teams are separated by a contest that can feel closer to a lottery than to sport. A single slip, a goalkeeper's lucky guess, and years of preparation evaporate.

Yet many argue the shoot-out is the purest test of nerve in all of sport. The walk from the halfway line to the penalty spot has been described by players as the longest of their lives. Some of the greatest names in football history have missed at this moment; some unknown players have become national heroes. That unpredictability, supporters say, is exactly why we watch.
Adapted from a sports feature, June 2026
  • ★ How many minutes are played before a shoot-out?
  • ★★ The writer compares the shoot-out to "a lottery". What does this comparison suggest?
  • ★★★ The text gives two opposite views of shoot-outs. Summarise each in one sentence, then say which you find more convincing.
Answers
1) 120 minutes. 2) That outcomes feel random/down to luck rather than skill. 3) View A: cruel and lottery-like, undeserved for the effort; View B: the ultimate test of nerve, thrilling precisely because unpredictable. Opinion free but must be justified.

Reading — Why does Britain have four teams?

To outsiders it looks bizarre: the United Kingdom is a single sovereign state, yet it sends four separate teams — England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland — to international football. The explanation is historical. The four British football associations are the oldest in the world, founded in the nineteenth century before FIFA even existed. When the modern game was codified, each nation already had its own federation, and they kept them.

This arrangement is not just administrative; it is deeply tied to identity. For many Scots and Welsh, supporting the national football team is a way of expressing a distinct nationhood within the UK. The terms can be confusing, though: "Great Britain" is an island, the "United Kingdom" is a state, and the "British Isles" is a purely geographical grouping that also includes the entirely independent Republic of Ireland.
Adapted from a culture feature, June 2026
  • ★★ Give the historical reason the UK has four football teams.
  • ★★ Distinguish, in your own words: Great Britain / United Kingdom / British Isles.
  • ★★★ The text links football to "identity" and "nationhood". Explain that connection, and compare it with a similar case you know (e.g. Catalonia, Brittany, a regional team).
Answers
1) The four British associations predate FIFA (19th c.); each already had its own federation and retained it. 2) GB = the island (England+Scotland+Wales); UK = the sovereign state (GB + Northern Ireland); British Isles = geographical island group, incl. the independent Republic of Ireland. 3) Open — reward a clear identity/sport link and a relevant comparison.
★★★ Mini-debate
"Every nation or region with a strong identity should have its own international football team." Prepare two arguments for and two against. Debate in groups of four.
Note
Useful for B2 oral fluency. Encourage hedging language from Day 2 ("it could be argued that…").

Reading — Anatomy of a match report

[TEAM A] survive shoot-out to break [TEAM B] hearts

For an hour in [CITY] it had looked comfortable. [TEAM A] were a goal up and controlling the tempo, until a moment of madness changed everything: a needless foul, a converted penalty, and suddenly the tie was level. [TEAM B], who had barely threatened all night, sensed blood. Extra time settled nothing. And so it came down to the cruellest lottery of all, where [PLAYER]'s decisive save sent [TEAM A] through and left [TEAM B] to contemplate what might have been.
Model report — replace the [brackets] with a real match.
Setup
Fill brackets with a real, dramatic Round-of-32 tie (ideally one that went to extra time/penalties). Note the past perfect ("it had looked", "who had barely threatened") for sequencing — draw attention to it.
  • ★★ Find one example of the past perfect and explain why the writer uses it here.
  • ★★★ Identify three techniques that make this report dramatic (e.g. word choice, sentence length, metaphor).
Answers
Past perfect: "it had looked comfortable" / "who had barely threatened" — to set the earlier background before the turning point. Techniques: emotive lexis ("moment of madness", "break hearts"); the metaphor "cruellest lottery"; short punchy clause "Extra time settled nothing"; the build-up/reversal structure.

Day 5 — The tournament so far, revisited

Theme: synthesising the week · Grammar: present perfect vs. past simple (the real contrast)
🎲 Move & speak — 2 games to start
1 · Four Corners — deserve vs will — Two rounds, same teams: first "who WILL win", then "who DESERVES to win". Pupils notice when their feet move between rounds and explain why.
Separates prediction from judgement.
2 · Line-up — on current form — Rank all remaining teams as a human line, defended with present-perfect evidence and one counter-argument each. English only.
Feeds directly into the structured debate.

Grammar focus — Why the present perfect matters here

Finished time → past simple · unfinished tournament → present perfect
Past simplePresent perfect
"Argentina beat Nigeria on Tuesday." (a finished match, a definite past time)"Argentina have won all their games so far." (the tournament is still going)
"He scored in the first round.""He has scored four goals this tournament."
Rule of thumb: if the time period (the World Cup) isn't over yet, use the present perfect. If you name a finished moment (Tuesday, the first half), use the past simple.
★★★ Past simple or present perfect?
Complete with the correct tense. Your teacher supplies the real names.
1. ___ (knock out) ___ yesterday in extra time.
2. So far, ___ (not concede) a single goal.
3. ___ (already / reach) the quarter-finals.
4. In the first half, ___ (look) nervous.
Answers
1) knocked out (past — "yesterday"). 2) have/has not conceded (present perfect — "so far"). 3) have/has already reached. 4) looked (past — "in the first half").
★★★ Structured debate — Who deserves to win?
Distinguish "who will win" (prediction) from "who deserves to win" (judgement). In groups, build a case for one team using:
  • present perfect evidence ("they have conceded only one goal");
  • calibrated predictions from Day 2 ("they are likely to…");
  • at least one counter-argument you then rebut.
Each group delivers a 60-second pitch; the class votes.
