Reading — Writing the news before the news is in

At the time of writing — the evening of Friday 27 June — the group stage had almost, but not entirely, concluded. A large number of sides had already booked their place in the Round of 32: the host nations, along with Brazil, France, Argentina, Germany and the Netherlands, were all safely through. For them, the only remaining question was who they would face.

Elsewhere, the picture remained stubbornly unresolved. Several groups still had fixtures to play, and — because of the new format — the eight "best third-placed teams" could not be confirmed until the very last whistle. Scotland's fate was typical of the suspense: they could still squeeze through as one of those third-placed sides, provided other results fell their way; equally, a single goal elsewhere could send them home. Any report written tonight, in other words, would be partly out of date by tomorrow.
Written on the evening of 27 June 2026, with several results still unknown.
  • ★★ Explain, in your own words, why "any report written tonight would be partly out of date by tomorrow".
  • ★★ What makes the third-placed teams so hard to confirm?
  • ★★★ The writer uses careful, hedged language throughout. Find three expressions that signal uncertainty, and explain the effect.
Answers
1) Because results that night would change the table — undecided places would be settled, so the snapshot would no longer be accurate. 2) The eight best thirds depend on comparing teams across all groups, which can't be done until every group has finished. 3) e.g. "remained stubbornly unresolved", "could not be confirmed until", "could still squeeze through", "provided other results fell their way", "could send them home". Effect: accuracy/honesty about what isn't yet known; avoids overclaiming.

Grammar focus — the language of "not yet known"

Matching certainty to structure
SituationLanguage
Confirmed"They have qualified / have already gone through."
Strong likelihood"They are likely to advance." / "They should go through."
Open possibility"They could / might still qualify."
Conditional"If they win tonight, they will go through." (real) · "If they were to lose, they would be out." (less likely)
