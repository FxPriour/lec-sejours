Where are we going?

Bath is a city in England, famous for its old golden-stone buildings and, most of all, for something the Romans built almost 2,000 years ago: a huge bathing complex fed by a natural hot spring.

In the morning, we'll visit The Roman Baths. In the afternoon, a guided tour continues the Roman theme around the city.

The Romans and the magic water

Long before the Romans arrived, local people believed a goddess called Sulis lived in a spring of hot water that bubbled up from the ground. When the Romans came, they connected Sulis to their own goddess, Minerva — so the site became sacred to Sulis Minerva, goddess of wisdom.

🔥 Fun fact: the spring water comes out of the ground at 46°C — hotter than the bath you take at home!

The Romans built a temple and bathing complex around the spring. People travelled long distances to bathe there, pray to the goddess, and ask her for help.

🪙 Fun fact: Romans threw coins, jewellery, and even little notes into the water as gifts to the goddess. Some notes asked her to punish someone who had stolen something from them — we call these curses!

For stronger students

A few extra details you can look out for and mention in the debrief tomorrow:

  • Aquae Sulis — the Roman name for Bath. It means "the waters of Sulis."
  • Curse tablets — notes to the goddess, written on thin sheets of lead, sometimes written backwards so only she could read them.
  • The Gorgon's head — a huge stone carving of a face with a moustache, above the entrance to the temple. Nobody is completely sure who it represents.
  • Hypocaust — the Roman underfloor heating system: hot air moved under raised floors to heat the rooms.
