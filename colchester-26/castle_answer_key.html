Colchester Castle — Discovery Challenge: Answer Key

For teacher use only · Not to be distributed to students
✓ Green — definitive answer (single correct response based on panels or historical record)
~ Blue — model/suggested answer (open question; accept any reasoned response)
👁 Amber — observation-based (answers depend on what students find; most likely responses listed)
Stop 1
Outside the Castle
Q1 ★★ What material is the castle mainly made from?
✓ Definitive answer
Stone — specifically a mix of Roman tile (thin red bricks, visible in the lower courses of the walls) and Norman rubble stone. Students should mention both stone and the visible Roman tiles.
📝 Point out the thin red Roman bricks before the visit — it makes this question much more satisfying to answer.
Q2 ★★ How old is the castle approximately?
✓ Definitive answer
✓ 900 years (the Norman keep was built around 1076, so approximately 950 years old). The Roman temple beneath it is around 2,000 years old — if students tick that, it's worth discussing why.
📝 The "2,000 years" distractor is partly true (the foundations are Roman), which makes for a good teaching moment.
Q3 ★★★ Why do you think the castle was built to look so strong?
~ Model answer
The Normans built the castle to control the local population after the 1066 conquest, to defend against potential attack, and to display their power and authority. Its massive walls and elevated position made it a symbol of Norman dominance as much as a military fortress. Accept any answer mentioning defence, power, or control.
Stop 2
The Roman Foundations
Q4 ★★ What was the Roman name of Colchester?
✓ Definitive answer
Camulodunum. Written on several panels inside the museum and in the trail leaflet. Straightforward retrieval.
Q5 ★★ Why was this city important to the Romans?
✓ Definitive answer
Colchester (Camulodunum) was the first Roman capital of Britain and the site of the most important Roman temple in Britain — the Temple of Claudius. It was a settlement of Roman veterans and the political and symbolic centre of Roman power in Britain before London (Londinium) grew in importance.
Q6 ★★★ Find one Roman object and describe it.
👁 Observation-based — answers will vary
Key objects in the museum include: the Colchester Vase (decorated pottery vessel), the Colchester Sphinx (Roman funerary sculpture), the Fenwick Hoard (gold and silver jewellery), Roman mosaic fragments, coins, burial cists, and glassware. Accept any accurately described object.
📝 If students are stuck, direct them to the Fenwick Hoard or the Colchester Sphinx — both have good panels and are visually striking.
Q6b ★★★ Challenge: find the oldest thing in this room.
👁 Observation-based — answers will vary
Likely: Iron Age objects pre-dating the Roman conquest (Celtic coins or Iron Age pottery). Accept any well-reasoned answer where the student has read the dating on the panel.
Stop 3
Boudica and the Revolt
Q7 ★ Who was Boudica?
✓ Definitive answer
✓ A British queen who fought the Romans. Specifically, queen of the Iceni tribe in eastern Britain. She led a major uprising against Roman rule around AD 60–61, destroying Camulodunum, Londinium, and Verulamium before being defeated.
Q8 ★★ Why did she attack Colchester?
✓ Definitive answer (any two reasons acceptable)
Accept any two of: (1) Colchester was the Roman capital and the Temple of Claudius was seen as the ultimate symbol of oppression; (2) Roman veterans had settled there and mistreated the local Britons; (3) the temple had been financed by extracting money from local inhabitants; (4) after the death of her husband, the Romans seized Iceni lands and mistreated her family.
Q9 ★★★ Do you think her rebellion was justified? Why?
~ Model answer (open question)
No right answer. A strong response acknowledges both perspectives: the genuine grievances of the Britons (mistreatment, exploitation, cultural suppression) and the scale of destruction caused (between 70,000–80,000 people killed). Encourage students to distinguish between the legitimacy of the cause and the methods used.
📝 The museum itself poses this question in a display. Good moment to discuss the complexity of historical judgment.
Stop 4
Norman Power
Q10 ★★ Who ordered the building of the castle?
✓ Definitive answer
King William I of England (William the Conqueror), around 1076. Clearly stated on museum panels.
Q11 ★★ Why did the Normans build castles in England? (tick 2)
✓ Definitive answer
✓ To defend against attack + ✓ To show power (and/or To control people — all three are defensible, accept any two of these three). "To grow food" is the distractor.
📝 If a student ticks "To grow food", ask them why — it usually reveals a misconception worth addressing.
Q12 ★★★ How is Norman architecture different from Roman architecture?
👁 Observation-based
Roman: thin red brick tiles, smaller arched spans, concrete. Norman: large cut stone blocks, rounded Romanesque arches, thick walls, small windows. Students can see both in the castle walls — the Roman bricks are visibly distinct from the Norman stonework above.
Stop 5
The Prison Cells
Q13 ★★ How were prisoners treated?
✓ Definitive answer
Conditions were very harsh — cold, damp cells with minimal food and light. The castle held prisoners notably during the 17th century, including Protestant martyrs during Mary I's reign and people accused of witchcraft by Matthew Hopkins, the "Witchfinder General". The museum has a recreated gaol with silhouettes of Protestant prisoners.
📝 The religious persecution angle is particularly vivid in the museum — mention it if students seem engaged.
Q14 ★★ Would you like to spend one night here? Explain.
~ Model answer (open question)
Accept any well-explained answer. Strong responses reference specific conditions observed (cold, dark, damp, cramped) rather than just "no, it's horrible".
Q15 ★★★ Find one surprising fact about prison life here.
👁 Observation-based — answers will vary
Likely answers: (1) some prisoners were held for religious beliefs, not crimes; (2) Matthew Hopkins was responsible for more executions than in the previous 100 years combined; (3) the jailer's daughter Mary Ann Smith lived her whole life at the castle and planted a sycamore tree in 1815 that is still there today; (4) during WWII the Roman vaults were used as an air raid shelter.
📝 The sycamore tree story and the WWII shelter use are particularly popular — they feel genuinely unexpected.
Stop 6
Final Reflection
⚠ Roof access confirmed suspended as of January 2026. Q17 is rephrased as a prediction/imagination task in the student sheet.
Q16 ★★ Why was this castle built in this location?
✓ Definitive answer
Built on the foundations of the Roman Temple of Claudius, providing ready-made foundations and building materials in an area where natural stone was scarce. The Normans also chose this symbolically powerful site to assert their authority as successors to Roman power. The elevated position offered a defensive advantage within the town.
Q17 ★★ What do you think you would be able to see from the top?
~ Model answer (imagination task — roof inaccessible)
Expected: the town of Colchester, Castle Park and its gardens, the surrounding Essex countryside, the town walls, church spires, and on a clear day possibly the River Colne. Accept any plausible answer.
📝 If Cambridge is coming up, encourage students to draw on what they imagine from tall Cambridge college buildings.
Q18 ★★★ If you were a Norman king/queen, would you choose this castle as your base?
~ Model answer (open question)
A strong answer weighs advantages (massive walls, symbolic importance, Roman foundations, central location in former capital) against disadvantages (cold, damp, far from London once it became the capital). Accept any reasoned response.

General notes for the debrief

Key vocabulary to check: Camulodunum · rebellion · justified · foundations · artefact · conquest · occupation

Best discussion starters: Q9 (Boudica: hero or rebel?) and Q14 (spend a night in the prison?) consistently generate strong oral production. Q18 (choose this castle?) works well as a closing whole-class vote.

Grammar link: The visit generates natural reported speech material — after the visit, ask students to report what the guide said or what they read on a panel. This feeds directly into the Day 2 debrief session.
