Reading

Bowling is one of the most popular recreational sports in the United States. An estimated 70 million Americans bowl at least once a year, making it the country's most participated-in sport after swimming. Bowling alleys are a fixture of American suburban life — air-conditioned, brightly lit, serving burgers and sodas.

The sport has ancient origins: archaeologists have found evidence of bowling-like games in ancient Egypt, dating back 5,000 years. But the modern game — with its ten-pin format, heavy balls, and electronic scoring — was largely shaped in 20th-century America. It became a working-class leisure activity, associated with unions, social clubs, and the culture of Middle America.

Compare this to darts, the quintessentially British pub game. Both involve precision, both require concentration, and both have managed to turn a casual leisure activity into a professional sport. But their cultural contexts could hardly be more different: the dimly lit, noisy pub versus the bright, climate-controlled bowling alley. Two sports, two cultures, one shared love of competition.
