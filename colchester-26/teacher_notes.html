Session 1 — Who am I? Input / grammar
0–10 min Name game warm-up
10–20 min Grammar focus (board)
20–35 min Identity card (Task 1)
35–55 min Partner interview (Task 2)
55–70 min Spot the difference (Task 3)
70–80 min Class presentation (Task 4)
80–85 min Vocabulary + bridge

Pedagogical intent

  • First session of the stay: the priority is making students speak immediately in English, with zero stakes.
  • The name game serves a dual purpose — language activation and learning classmates' names (useful for the bingo on the train, which some may not have completed).
  • The identity card is deliberately easy to give every student an early win.
  • Tasks 2–4 form a progression: closed info gathering → comparison → public production. Don't skip Task 3 — the connector work (whereas/while/both) is reused in Day 8.

Differentiation

LevelKey adjustments
★ A1/A2Allow L1 as a bridge. Key words on the card rather than sentences. For Task 4, reading from notes is allowed.
★★ B1Encourage full sentences in Task 2. Push for at least one indirect question.
★★★ B2Task 4 without notes. Task 3 with whereas/while. Encourage elaboration beyond the prompt.
💡 Use this session to observe oral fluency and adjust group composition if needed. Placement tests don't always predict oral confidence.
Session 2 — Meet & Greet Oral application
0–5 min Vocab check
5–30 min Host family roleplay (3 rounds)
30–45 min Forbidden words game
45–65 min Journal (Day 1)
65–70 min Closing round

Pedagogical intent

  • The roleplay directly prepares students for the evening — they will meet their host family in a few hours. This is functional English with immediate real-world stakes.
  • The observer role is important: it gives students a structured way to give peer feedback in English and keeps the third student engaged during the roleplay.
  • The forbidden words game is deliberately light — it ends the session on a playful note before the emotionally loaded moment of arriving in a new family.
  • The closing round ("Tonight I will try to say…") is a commitment device — make sure every student says something specific, not just "hello."
⚠ Some students will be anxious about the host family arrival. Don't minimise that — acknowledge it briefly, then redirect to the practical tools the session gives them.

Day 4 — Friday 19 June · Getting ready for Cambridge

Curriculum axisLe village / la ville · Institutions

Student filestudent_J4_cambridge_prep.html + student_J5_cambridge.html (observation log, used live on the day)
Session — Cambridge prep + comparatives intro Input / grammar
0–5 min Vocab check (Day 3 words)
5–25 min Reading (Oxbridge college system)
25–40 min Grammar focus + exercise
40–50 min Cambridge mission briefing
50–60 min Vocab + predictions journal

Pedagogical intent

  • The Hogwarts comparison is the hook — virtually every French student knows Harry Potter, and the college system genuinely resembles it. Use this to get students talking before the grammar.
  • The French vs UK university system contrast is explicitly pedagogical: students are in seconde and many have never thought about higher education. This is a rare chance to make that concrete and relevant.
  • Comparatives are introduced here rather than on the darts day because Cambridge provides richer, more natural comparison material (old vs new, large vs small, residential vs commuter, prestigious vs accessible). Day 8 (bowling) reviews and extends this with sport-based comparisons.
  • The "predictions" journal entry creates a cross-day loop: students write predictions here, then check them in their Cambridge evening journal.

Differentiation

LevelKey adjustments
★ A1/A2Comprehension questions 1–2. Grammar exercise: simple sentences only ("Cambridge is older than my school"). Mission: one item to find.
★★ B1Full reading + grammar exercise. Use whereas in at least one sentence.
★★★ B2Not nearly as… as + both…and. Question 4 (Hogwarts) developed in full.
💡 The observation log (student_J5_cambridge.html) is used live during the excursion — remind students to bring it on their phone or as a printout. It feeds directly into the Saturday evening journal.

10–25 min Reading + 3 questions
25–35 min Modal verbs revision (board)
35–80 min Written assessment (45 min)

Pedagogical intent

  • The weekend debrief is crucial after two days away from class — it re-activates English and gives you intelligence on how students are getting on with their families.
  • The assessment is placed mid-stay (Day 5 of 8) to allow correction feedback before the end of the trip. It covers the grammar of the first four days: present perfect, reported speech, passives, modals.
  • The environmental text is chosen to pre-teach vocabulary students will need for the assessment's production section, without being the same text as the assessment itself.
  • The Crazy Golf link is intentional — the text argues that outdoor leisure spaces are genuinely beneficial, which frames the afternoon activity as educationally meaningful rather than just fun.
⚠ Assessment logistics: print evaluation_J6.html separately. No documents allowed during the 45-minute window. Two differentiated writing tasks (★ and ★★★) — make sure students know they choose one, not both.

Assessment overview (evaluation_J6.html)

  • Part A (10 pts) — Reading comprehension: authentic text on UK biodiversity + 6 questions (★ to ★★★)
  • Part B (10 pts) — Language: tense completion + passive transformation + modal fill-in
  • Part C (10 pts) — Written production: two-tier choice (★ 40–60 words / ★★★ 120 words essay)
Session 2 — Sustainable leisure design Oral application + production
0–15 min Assessment collective feedback
15–40 min Sustainable leisure space (group task)
40–60 min Halfway journal

Pedagogical intent

  • The collective feedback on the assessment should focus on patterns (common errors across the group), not individual marks. Keep it light and forward-looking.
  • The group design task is deliberately open-ended after a structured assessment — it rewards creativity and gives students agency after 45 minutes of constrained writing.
  • The "halfway" journal is a metacognitive moment: after 5 days, students should be able to articulate what they've learned and what still feels difficult. Don't rush this.

Pedagogical intent

  • The "before you watch" observation task is key — it transforms passive viewing into active note-taking. Check that every student has noted their three observations before leaving for the cinema.
  • Hedged opinions ("I think/felt/found it…", "It could have been…") are introduced here because they are the register students need for the review task and for discussing opinions generally. This feeds directly into Session 2 and also into Day 8's final presentation.
  • The text about the Toy Story franchise is chosen to give context without spoilers for TS5 — it frames the film as part of a cultural phenomenon rather than just a cartoon.

0–5 min First reactions round
5–30 min Group review pitch (prep + delivery)
30–50 min Written film review
50–60 min Journal

Pedagogical intent

  • The class vote after pitches ("which review was most convincing?") adds stakes and a genuine communicative purpose — students are persuading, not just describing.
  • The written review consolidates the oral pitch: students have already structured their argument, so the writing should feel manageable even for lower levels.
💡 If the film overruns or the group is tired from the outing, the written review can become homework (journal entry) rather than in-class production. The oral pitch is the priority.

30–45 min Grammar focus (obligation modals)

Pedagogical intent

  • The warm-up ("I can/can't/love/hate swimming") serves a dual safety purpose: it identifies non-swimmers discreetly before the session, without putting anyone on the spot in front of the group.
  • The safety briefing read-aloud is a genuine safety exercise, not just a language activity. Every student must demonstrate comprehension. Use translation (student reads in English, another confirms the French meaning) rather than asking "does everyone understand?" — that always gets a yes.
  • The modal verbs here (must/mustn't vs should vs don't have to) revisit the same structures from Day 5 in a different, more urgent register — safety. This consolidation is intentional.
⚠ LEC supervision guidelines apply. Identify non-swimmers before departure. Assign one adult to poolside at all times. The "don't have to swim" rule is important — do not pressure students who feel uncomfortable.

50–60 min Journal

Pedagogical intent

  • The wellbeing discussion covers all four sports of the trip (darts, crazy golf, swimming, bowling tomorrow) — it's a rehearsal for the Day 8 comparative review in a lower-stakes format.
  • By Day 7, students should be able to sustain a 10-minute group discussion with minimal scaffolding. Use this session to observe and note who is ready to present alone in Day 8.

Pedagogical intent

  • The "very British vs very American" framing is a genuine cultural contrast that students find engaging — it also connects to the World Cup session (if taught) where UK/US geography has been discussed.

Session 2 — Final presentations + farewell letter Oral production + final writing
0–5 min Bowling debrief
5–35 min Final presentations (2 min each)
35–55 min Farewell letter (journal)
55–60 min Closing round

Pedagogical intent

  • The final presentation is the summative oral production of the stay. Topics are deliberately open ("what I take away", "one thing England does better", etc.) to allow genuine personal reflection rather than rehearsed answers.
  • For A1/A2 students, the farewell letter can replace the oral presentation — it's a more supported format that still produces genuine, meaningful output.
  • The closing round ("When I get home, the first thing I will say in English is…") ends the stay on a forward-looking note — English doesn't stop here. Keep it light; let the last word be funny if it wants to be.
💡 The farewell letter is real. Encourage students to give it to their host family on their last evening or the following morning. Even a short handwritten note matters enormously to the host family.

Session 1 — Dates, times and time zones Input / grammar
0–5 min Warm-up: what do you know?
5–20 min Reading (3-country tournament)
20–35 min Time zone table + questions
35–45 min Grammar focus (time expressions)
45–55 min FIFA host cities map task
55–60 min Vocabulary

Pedagogical intent

  • The time zone table is genuinely useful information for students — several England and France matches fall during the stay and students may watch them with their host families. The question about France vs Norway on departure day (Fri 26 June) is the most personally resonant.
  • The FIFA map task (cities unlabelled) is a geography challenge — students locate host cities across North America without place-name scaffolding. Pair with the PDF map for best effect.
  • The "by the time" structure is particularly rich here: "By the time we get home, France will have played two matches." This naturally bridges time zones and future tense.
Session 2 — British Isles geography + predictions Oral application
0–20 min British Isles geography (text + map colouring)
20–30 min World Cup nations sorting task
30–45 min Grammar: predictions + prediction pool
45–55 min England's chances discussion
55–60 min Journal prompt

Pedagogical intent

  • The UK/GB/British Isles/England distinction is a perennial confusion for French students. Scotland competing as a separate team from England at the same World Cup is the ideal hook — it makes the distinction immediately concrete and memorable.
  • The british_isles.pdf colouring map should be printed in advance (one per student). It takes about 10 minutes to complete and gives students a reference they can keep.
  • The prediction pool ("no money involved!") is deliberately framed as fun — use it. Have students share their predictions at the start of each day and update them as results come in. This can become a recurring thread throughout the stay.
  • The "England's chances" discussion is an excellent host family conversation topic. Coach students to ask their host family that evening — almost every English adult will have an opinion, and it generates far more natural conversation than asking about the weather.
💡 Update the prediction pool results at the start of subsequent sessions as matches are played. Two minutes of "what happened last night?" is highly motivating and requires no preparation.
