Reading — A different kind of university

Cambridge isn't really one big university campus — it's made up of more than 30 separate colleges, each with its own buildings, dining hall, library, gardens, and traditions. Most colleges have between 300 and 500 students, studying many different subjects and at different levels.

Students belong to both the university (which awards the degree and organises lectures and exams) and a college (which provides accommodation, meals, a social life, and small-group teaching sessions called supervisions). If you've read or watched Harry Potter, the system might remind you of Hogwarts: students live, eat, and socialise within their "house" — or in this case, college — but attend lessons with students from across the whole school.

This is very different from the French system, where students usually attend one campus, follow lectures (CM) and seminars (TD) organised by their UFR, and don't live in residential colleges with their own identity and traditions. Oxford works in almost exactly the same way as Cambridge — the two universities are so similar, and their rivalry so old, that people often call them "Oxbridge".
